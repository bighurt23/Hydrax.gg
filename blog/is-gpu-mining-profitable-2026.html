Is GPU Mining Still Profitable in 2026?

Mining guide · ~6 min read · by the Hydrax.GG team

Short version: yes — but only if your electricity is cheap and you do the math first. GPU mining didn't die when Ethereum moved to proof-of-stake; it just got more sensitive to three numbers. Get those right and a rig still prints a modest, steady return. Get them wrong and you're paying the power company to heat your room.

This guide walks through exactly what decides profitability in 2026, then shows you how to check your specific rig in about a minute with our free mining profit calculator.

The three numbers that decide everything

Forget the hype and the doom takes. Mining profit is just one equation:

Profit = Coin Revenue − Electricity Cost − (Hardware ÷ Lifespan)

  1. Coin revenue — your hashrate ÷ the network's hashrate, times the daily block rewards, times the coin's price. This moves every day with price and difficulty.
  2. Electricity cost — your watts × 24h × your $/kWh. This is the silent profit-killer, and it's the one number most people guess wrong.
  3. Hardware payback — what you paid for the card spread over how long it earns. Cheaper cards (hello, used GPUs) pay back far faster.

What's actually worth mining on a GPU in 2026?

It rotates constantly with price and difficulty, but the usual GPU contenders are:

  • Ravencoin (RVN) — KawPow, the classic GPU coin, ASIC-resistant.
  • Ethereum Classic (ETC) — Etchash, high hashrate, big network.
  • Ergo (ERG) — Autolykos, memory-friendly and efficient.
  • Monero (XMR) — RandomX, mined on your CPU (great as a free bonus alongside a GPU coin).

New vs. used GPUs: the ROI math

This is the lever most newcomers miss. Mining revenue per card is identical whether you bought it new or used — so a cheaper card simply pays itself back faster. A renewed card at half the price can halve your breakeven time. If you're buying to mine, used / Amazon Renewed GPUs are almost always the better ROI play (and many ship with a warranty).

Don't forget the hidden costs

  • Pool fees (~1%) and the odd stale share.
  • Heat & wear — fans and risers die; budget for spares.
  • Downtime — no rig runs 100%; we default the calculator to 98% uptime.
  • Difficulty creep — as more miners join, your share shrinks. Our ROI calculator can model that so your payback estimate isn't fantasy.

The honest bottom line

GPU mining in 2026 is a thin-margin, cheap-power game, not a get-rich-scheme. With sub-$0.10 electricity and well-priced (ideally used) hardware, a tuned rig earns a steady little return and doubles as a heater. With expensive power, it's a hobby that costs money.
