What RVN, NEOX, XNA and FREN have in common

All four use KawPow, a member of the ProgPoW family designed to keep mining on consumer GPUs instead of specialized ASICs. That matters for home miners: it means a gaming graphics card stays competitive, the playing field isn't owned by warehouse farms, and the same miner and the same tuning carry across every coin on the list.

Why mine these coins on a GPU in 2026

  • GPU-friendly by design. KawPow resists ASICs, so a single modern NVIDIA card earns a fair share instead of being priced out.
  • One skill, four coins. Profitability shifts with price and difficulty, so being able to hop between RVN, NEOX, XNA and FREN lets you follow whichever pays best for your card today.
  • Dual-mine for free. Your CPU sits mostly idle while the GPU mines. Adding Monero on the CPU is essentially free extra income on the same rig and the same power supply.
  • Low barrier to entry. No special hardware, no firmware, no warehouse. If you own a gaming PC, you already own a miner.

What you need before you start

  1. An NVIDIA GPU with enough memory for the coin's DAG — RTX 30, 40 and 50 series all work well.
  2. A wallet address for each coin you want to mine, so the pool has somewhere to send payouts. Each coin has its own address format, so don't reuse one coin's address for another.
  3. A mining pool for the coin (more on this below).
  4. A miner. We'll use the free HydraX miner because it mines all four KawPow coins and can dual-mine Monero from the same program — but the steps generalize to any KawPow miner.

Step 1: Pick a pool

Unless you have a large farm, you'll mine on a pool rather than solo — pooled payouts are small but steady instead of rare and random. For these coins, established pools publish a stratum address and port you point your miner at, then attach your wallet address as the username. Step 3: Edit the config with your coin, pool and wallet

Mining setup is almost always the same three facts: which coin, which pool, which wallet. Open the config file and set the coin (RVN, NEOX, XNA or FREN), paste the pool's address and port, and paste your wallet address for that coin. Add a worker name if you run more than one rig so you can tell them apart on the pool dashboard.

Switching coins later is the same edit in reverse: change the coin and pool lines to the next KawPow coin, point at that coin's pool, drop in that coin's wallet address, and restart. Nothing about your hardware or tuning changes.

Step 4 (optional): Turn on Monero to dual-mine

This is the part most beginners miss. Your GPU mines the KawPow coin; your CPU is free to mine Monero (XMR) with RandomX at the same time. Because the two use completely different hardware, you earn on a second coin for almost no loss on the first — the GPU coin and Monero run together out of one miner. Set your XMR pool and wallet alongside the GPU coin, pick the dual mode, and a single rig now earns on two coins from one power bill.

Step 5: Start it and confirm accepted shares

Launch the miner and watch the console. Within a minute or two you should see it connect to the pool, build the DAG, and start reporting a hashrate. The number that matters is accepted shares — each one is proof your rig contributed valid work and is earning. If you turned on dual mining, you'll see both the GPU coin's shares and Monero's shares scrolling by. Once you cross the pool's minimum payout, the balance lands in your wallet.

Step 6: Tune for hashrate per watt

Stock settings are rarely optimal for mining. KawPow is fairly core-clock sensitive, while the memory clock matters too — but the real goal is hashrate per watt, not raw hashrate, because watts are your power bill. The high-value levers:

  • Power limit — capping power usually trims only a little hashrate while cutting heat and electricity cost. Start here.
  • Core clock — KawPow leans on core; a modest, stable core offset can lift hashrate. Push gradually and back off at the first invalid share.
  • Memory clock — helps too, but don't chase it past stability; errors cost you more uptime than a few extra megahashes are worth.
  • Temperatures — watch core and memory temps and keep airflow strong. A rig stable at slightly lower clocks beats one that crashes every few hours.

Change one thing at a time, let it run, and confirm shares are still accepted before the next tweak. For the broader approach, see our beginner's guide to mining.

Step 7: Track it like a business

A coin that's profitable today can flip red after a price drop or a difficulty jump, so a one-time check isn't enough. Log what each rig earns, what you pay in electricity, and your real net over time — that's the difference between mining on hope and mining on numbers.
