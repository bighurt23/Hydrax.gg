What makes mining software "good"

Ignore the flashy claims and judge on five things that genuinely matter:

  • Supported algorithms. Your coin runs a specific algorithm — KawPow (Ravencoin and its forks), Etchash (Ethereum Classic), RandomX (Monero), and so on. The miner has to support it. That's the first filter.
  • Supported hardware. NVIDIA, AMD, or CPU. Some miners do all three; some specialize. Match it to what you own.
  • Dev fee. Nearly every miner takes a small built-in fee, usually around 1%, by briefly mining to the developer. About 1% is normal; far higher or hidden fees are a red flag.
  • Stability and efficiency. A miner that's slightly faster but crashes every few hours earns less than a steady one, because uptime is everything. Hashrate per watt matters more than raw hashrate.
  • Ease of setup. Some miners need hand-written config files; others run from a simple launch script. For beginners this is the difference between mining tonight and giving up.

The established free GPU miners

If you're mining on a graphics card, these are the well-known, free, mature options. They're closed-source, support a wide range of algorithms, and most work on both NVIDIA and AMD. They each take a small dev fee. We're not going to pretend they aren't good — they're the standard for a reason, and if you need broad AMD support or an algorithm we don't cover, they're the right call.

  • lolMiner — broad algorithm support across NVIDIA and AMD; widely used for Etchash and many others.
  • T-Rex — an NVIDIA-focused miner known for strong KawPow performance.
  • NBMiner — another long-running multi-algorithm miner for NVIDIA and AMD.

All three are free to download. Whichever you pick, only download from the project's official source and expect your antivirus to flag any miner — that's common, but stick to well-known software.

How to actually choose

  1. Start from your coin's algorithm. KawPow, Etchash, RandomX — this narrows the field immediately.
  2. Filter by your hardware. NVIDIA, AMD or CPU. Don't fight a miner that doesn't target your card.
  3. Check the dev fee and prefer something around 1% and transparent.
  4. Test stability over a day, not a minute. Watch accepted shares and temperatures.
  5. Run the numbers. Whatever miner you choose, profit comes down to hashrate, power draw and your electricity rate — check it in the profit calculator before you commit.
