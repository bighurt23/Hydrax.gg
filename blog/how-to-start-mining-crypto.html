How to Start Mining Crypto in 2026 (Beginner's Guide)

Guide · ~8 min read · by the Hydrax.GG team

Mining isn't magic internet money — it's a small business with hardware, an electricity bill, and a profit margin. Get the order of operations right and you can start this weekend, often with gear you already own.

We build mining software for a living, so here's the honest version: most beginners skip straight to "what should I buy?" and lose money before they've mined a single coin. The fix is doing one thing first — checking the math. This guide walks you through the whole path, in order, from the mining profit calculator to your first payout, without the hype.

Step 1: Check profitability first (don't buy blind)

This is the step everyone wants to skip and the one that matters most. Before you spend a dollar, you need three numbers: a hardware hashrate, its total power draw at the wall, and your real electricity rate in dollars per kilowatt-hour. Plug those into the profit calculator and you'll see whether a given rig nets a few dollars a day or quietly loses money to your power bill.

The single biggest variable is your electricity rate. The same GPU can be profitable at $0.10/kWh and a money pit at $0.30/kWh. Step 2: Pick your hardware path

There are three ways to mine, and they suit very different people. You don't need all three — pick the one that matches what you already have and what you're willing to spend.

GPUBeginners with a gaming PC; flexible across many coinsLower efficiency than ASICs; one card earns modestly
ASICSerious miners chasing one coin (e.g. Bitcoin)Expensive, loud, hot, and locked to one algorithm
CPUQuiet home mining on RandomX coins like MoneroLow earnings; only a handful of coins are CPU-friendly

If you already own a gaming PC, start there — your GPU is a working miner the moment you install software. To weigh the buy decision, read ASIC vs GPU mining and our roundup of the best mining GPUs in 2026. When you're ready to buy, used and renewed GPUs are usually the smartest first purchase because a lower price means faster payback.

Step 3: Pick a coin to mine

Your hardware narrows the field. ASICs are locked to a specific algorithm, GPUs can hop between many coins, and CPUs are limited to RandomX-style coins. Within those limits, you're choosing the coin that pays the most per day for your gear right now — and that changes constantly with price and difficulty.

Don't pick a coin off a forum thread and assume it's best. Sort by live profitability instead: our most-profitable-coin guide walks through how to evaluate options, and the calculator lets you compare what a given coin actually pays for your specific hashrate and power draw. As a beginner, favor a coin with an established pool and a wallet that's easy to set up.

Step 4: Set up a wallet for payouts

You need somewhere for mined coins to land. A wallet gives you a receiving address — a long string you'll paste into your pool or miner config so rewards have a destination. Your options, simplest to most secure:

  • Exchange wallet — fastest to set up and easy to cash out, but the exchange holds your keys. Fine for small amounts while you're learning.
  • Software wallet — a desktop or mobile app where you control the keys. A good middle ground for most home miners.
  • Hardware wallet — a physical device that keeps keys offline. Worth it once your balance is large enough to matter.

Whatever you choose, copy the receiving address carefully and back up your recovery phrase offline. A mistyped address means lost coins with no undo, and no support line can reverse it.

Step 5: Choose a pool (or solo mine)

Solo mining means you keep a full block reward — but only if your hardware happens to find a block, which for a single rig can take a very long time on a large network. Pool mining combines thousands of miners and splits rewards by contribution, so payouts are small but steady. Almost every beginner should start with a pool.

When comparing pools, look at the fee (usually 0.5–2%), the minimum payout, server locations near you, and the payout scheme. Lower fees are nice, but reliable uptime and a sane minimum payout matter more when you're just getting your first coins. You'll point your mining software at the pool's address and port, then attach your wallet address as the username.

Step 6: Install and configure mining software

The software is what turns your hardware into a miner. It connects to the pool, runs the coin's algorithm, and reports your hashrate and accepted shares. Setup usually comes down to a short config: the pool URL and port, your wallet address, and a worker name so you can tell rigs apart.

  1. Download a reputable miner for your coin's algorithm and verify the source. Your antivirus may flag miners — that's common, but only trust well-known software.
  2. Edit the config with your pool address, port, wallet address, and a worker name.
  3. Start it and watch the console for "accepted" shares — that's proof you're contributing work and earning.
  4. Confirm payouts appear on the pool dashboard once you cross its minimum threshold.

If the console shows accepted shares and your pool dashboard shows your worker online, congratulations — you're mining. Everything after this is optimization.

Step 7: Tune clocks and power, and watch temps

Out of the box, a GPU is rarely at its best for mining. The goal is more hashrate per watt, not just more hashrate — because watts are your power bill. The usual levers:

  • Power limit — capping power often barely dents hashrate while cutting electricity cost and heat. This is the highest-value tweak for most cards.
  • Memory clock — many coins are memory-bandwidth bound, so a stable memory overclock can lift hashrate directly. Push gradually and back off at the first sign of errors.
  • Core clock — often best left modest or slightly undervolted; chasing core speed past the point it helps just wastes watts and adds heat.
  • Temperatures — watch GPU core and memory temps. Sustained heat shortens hardware life, so prioritize airflow and reasonable limits over a few extra megahashes.

Change one setting at a time, let it run, and confirm shares are still accepted before moving on. A rig that's stable at a slightly lower hashrate beats one that crashes every few hours and loses uptime.

Step 8: Track it like a business

Mining once-profitable doesn't mean mining always-profitable. Prices swing, difficulty climbs, and your power bill arrives every month, so a snapshot goes stale fast. Treat it like a small business: log what you earn, what you spend on electricity and hardware, and your real net over time.

The free calculator tells you whether to start; ongoing bookkeeping tells you whether to keep going and what you owe at tax time. The honest caveats

Before you commit, go in clear-eyed. Hardware costs money up front and loses value over time. Electricity is a real, recurring bill that can erase your margin entirely at a high rate. Rigs run hot and loud — a single GPU is fine in a spare room, but several together need real ventilation and you'll hear them. And earnings are never guaranteed: a price drop or a difficulty jump can turn a green rig red overnight. None of this means don't mine — it means start small, mine with gear you already own if you can, and let the numbers, not the hype, decide when to scale.

Frequently asked

Do I need a special computer to start? No. A typical gaming PC with a modern GPU can start mining today, and some coins mine on a regular CPU. Run your exact setup through the calculator to see what it would earn before buying anything new.

Is GPU or ASIC better for a beginner? For most first-timers, a GPU — it's flexible across coins and you may already own one. ASICs earn more on their target coin but are costly, loud, and inflexible. Our ASIC vs GPU comparison lays out the tradeoffs.

How long until I see a payout? On a pool, usually within hours to days, once your contribution crosses the pool's minimum payout. Solo mining a large network can take far longer, which is why beginners start with a pool.
